# Psychedelics, Consciousness & Perception

Consciousness research has suffered from a measurement problem. The field has sophisticated theories about what consciousness might be, but its primary instruments — self-report questionnaires — capture only what people can articulate about their own experience. This program starts from the premise that conscious states have objective signatures in behavior and physiology, and that building the tools to detect those signatures is the central empirical challenge.

## Intellectual foundations

The lab's serotonin work produced pharmacological control over a neuromodulatory system known to reshape subjective experience. The decision-making work built precise behavioral paradigms and computational frameworks for measuring what the brain infers about its own states. And a sustained philosophical engagement with consciousness — from first-person experience to the irreducibility of subjective states (Mainen, 2000; Rigato, Rennie & Mainen, 2019; Gomez-Marin & Mainen, 2016) — made the step from studying decisions to studying experience a natural extension.

These threads converge on a methodology: instead of asking "what is consciousness?", ask what changes when consciousness changes, and build instruments precise enough to detect the answer.

## Psychedelics in mice

Brain-wide Neuropixels recordings in mice under LSD reveal that psychedelics restructure neural variability in a timescale-dependent way: trial-to-trial variability increases at fast timescales (~100 ms) while decreasing at slow timescales (1-10 s). This is a reorganization of the temporal structure of neural fluctuations, not a simple increase or decrease in noise. The preparation uses the IBL's standardized behavioral platform, allowing comparison with non-drug neural dynamics across the same brain regions.

## The measurement paradox

A systematic review of how altered states of consciousness have been measured across the published literature has uncovered a measurement paradox: despite studying dozens of different induction methods (meditation, psychedelics, anesthesia, hypnosis, sensory deprivation, dreaming, and more), the field converges on a small number of dominant questionnaires — and those instruments share substantial overlap in their item content. The field appears to be measuring a few dimensions repeatedly while believing it is measuring many different things.

The diagnosis identifies what existing tools actually measure, what they miss, and where new instruments are needed. It sets the stage for the lab's approach to measuring conscious states through behavior and physiology rather than self-report alone.

## Seeing

If conscious states vary, then perception should vary with them. The seeing project uses eye tracking and psychophysics to study how humans perceive visual textures — how gaze patterns, fixation sequences, and texture discrimination change across conditions and individuals. The work connects to the broader question of whether perceptual differences across people (and across states within a person) can be captured by objective behavioral measures.

A related line of work on saccadic imagery asks how the structure of eye movements during waking perception compares to those during hypnagogia, dreaming, and hallucination. Saccadic patterns during these states carry signatures of the underlying visual construction process — the sequence and timing of fixations reflect what the visual system is building, whether from retinal input or from memory and imagination.

## Tasting

The tasting project studies cross-modal perception of flavor — how visual information shapes the perception of taste and how mental imagery of flavor relates to actual gustatory experience. Flavor is one of the most thoroughly integrated percepts in human experience: it fuses gustation, olfaction, texture, temperature, and visual expectation into a single conscious representation. Studying how that integration works — and how it breaks down or shifts with context and individual differences — probes the mechanisms by which the brain constructs unified experience from multiple sensory channels. The work sits at the boundary between perception research and the consciousness program, testing whether the same objective behavioral measures that capture variation in visual experience generalize to interoceptive and multisensory domains.

## Audition and embodied experience

The perceptual program extends into hearing through work on auditory confidence and on whether specific auditory stimuli can measurably shift physiological and experiential states. The auditory confidence work, in collaboration with groups at BCCN Berlin and the Allen Institute, tests whether the computational framework developed for olfactory and visual confidence generalizes to a third modality. The sound healing work investigates a practice with wide cultural presence but little controlled study, asking whether its effects are empirically detectable and, if so, through what mechanisms.

## The philosophical thread

Running beneath the empirical work is a sustained philosophical engagement with the problem of consciousness. From an early essay on "Thinking about feeling" (Mainen, 2000) through work on expanding cognition beyond the neural (Gomez-Marin & Mainen, 2016) to the examination of free will and spontaneous action (Rigato, Rennie & Mainen, 2019), the lab has argued that consciousness must be confronted empirically by neuroscience — even when the available tools are inadequate. The philosophical work does not stand apart from the experiments; it motivates the specific measurement strategy the program pursues, and it grounds the claim that subjective experience is a legitimate target for objective investigation.

## Current directions

The program is converging on a concrete methodology: combine pharmacological and experiential manipulation of conscious states with high-dimensional behavioral measurement — eye tracking for gaze dynamics and saccadic structure, facial video for affective and autonomic signatures, physiological recording for heart rate, skin conductance, and respiration — and use computational modeling to extract state variables from these streams. The goal is to make the variation of conscious states empirically tractable: to detect, in the behavioral and physiological data, what changes when experience changes, without relying solely on what people say about their experience.
