# Decision-Making & Confidence

## Confidence as a neural computation

In 2008, Adam Kepecs reported an experiment that reframed how neuroscience thinks about decisions. Rats trained on an odor discrimination could, after committing to a choice, either wait for a reward or abandon the trial. On difficult trials — where the odor mixture was ambiguous — rats abandoned sooner, as if they knew their choice was unreliable. Recordings from orbitofrontal cortex (OFC) revealed the neural basis: a population of neurons that encoded the probability that a decision was correct, given the sensory evidence and the choice itself (Kepecs, Uchida, Zariwala & Mainen, 2008, *Nature*).

The computational framework that emerged from this experiment unified confidence with Bayesian decision theory: confidence is the posterior probability of being correct, conditioned on both evidence and decision identity (Kepecs & Mainen, 2012). The framework made specific, testable predictions about how confidence signals should vary with evidence strength, choice direction, and outcome history.

## OFC and the geometry of decisions

Claudia Feierstein's earlier recordings had already shown that OFC during odor-guided spatial navigation carries a rich, mixed-selective code — spatial goals, outcomes, and task variables represented jointly across the population (Feierstein et al., 2006). Michael Quirk identified networks of fast-spiking interneurons that organize these representations (Quirk et al., 2009). OFC, in this view, maintains a structured representation of the full decision problem: stimulus, action, and expected outcome bound together in a single neural space.

Armin Lak then tested whether OFC is necessary for confidence-guided behavior. OFC lesions impaired rats' ability to calibrate their waiting to decision difficulty, while leaving decision accuracy intact (Lak et al., 2014). The dissociation established confidence as a distinct computation — one that requires OFC — rather than a byproduct of the evidence accumulation that drives the choice.

## Confidence as Bayesian probability

Florent Meyniel synthesized the empirical findings with a broader theoretical claim: across species and paradigms, confidence operates as a Bayesian probability estimate, computed in parallel with the decision itself, represented in dedicated neural circuits, and deployed to guide subsequent behavior (Meyniel, Sigman & Mainen, 2015, *Neuron*). The review became an influential framework for the growing field of metacognition research, connecting rodent electrophysiology to human psychophysics under a single computational account.

## Voluntary action and the timing of decisions

A separate line of investigation addressed a different aspect of decision-making: the temporal structure of self-initiated actions. When no external stimulus triggers a choice, what determines when an animal acts?

Masayoshi Murakami's recordings from secondary motor cortex revealed ramping neural activity preceding self-initiated actions, with trial-by-trial variability in the ramp's time course predicting the timing of each movement (Murakami et al., 2014, *Nat Neurosci*). This variability carried structure: deterministic and stochastic components of the ramping process could be dissociated, each contributing differently to the distribution of action times (Murakami et al., 2017). The ramping dynamics provided a mechanistic account of how the brain generates temporally variable but statistically regular behavior.

Joana Rigato connected this work to philosophical questions about free will and spontaneous decision-making (Rigato, Murakami & Mainen, 2015). Stefano Recanatesi showed that metastable attractor dynamics can explain the variable timing of stable behavioral sequences — a computational mechanism that produces reliable order with unreliable timing (Recanatesi et al., 2021).

## Learning, reliability, and optimal coding

Jan Drugowitsch and Alexandre Pouget brought a complementary computational perspective: optimal decision-making requires learning the reliability of one's own sensory evidence, and both humans and animals adjust their speed-accuracy tradeoff based on accumulated experience (Drugowitsch et al., 2019; Mendonça et al., 2020). This collaboration also showed that Weber's law — the scaling of discrimination thresholds with stimulus magnitude — emerges as a consequence of optimal coding under neural resource constraints (Pardo-Vazquez et al., 2019).

## Sensorimotor decisions below cortex

Gidon Felsen's work on the superior colliculus opened a distinct question within the decision program: how far down the sensorimotor hierarchy does evidence representation extend? His recordings during locomotor decisions revealed that the superior colliculus — a structure traditionally associated with motor execution — carries representations of sensory evidence that evolve during the decision process (Felsen & Mainen, 2008, 2012). Decision-related computation, in other words, is distributed across the brain's action systems, not sequestered in cortex.

## Legacy

The decision-making and confidence program established that the brain evaluates its own decisions as it makes them. Confidence is computed in parallel with the choice, represented in dedicated circuitry (particularly OFC), and used to calibrate subsequent behavior. The Bayesian framework developed here — confidence as posterior probability — provided the formal language that connected rodent neurophysiology, human psychophysics, and computational theory into a unified account of metacognitive computation.
